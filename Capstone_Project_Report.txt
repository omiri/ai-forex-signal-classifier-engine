CAPSTONE PROJECT REPORT: 
AI-Driven Market Trend & Trading Signal Engine

1. Problem Statement
Retail traders, financial analysts, and portfolio managers consistently face the dual challenges of emotional bias and information overload. Financial markets produce a massive volume of tick data, and tracking multiple technical indicators (such as momentum, trend, and volatility) across varying timeframes manually is inefficient. This often leads to conflicting signals—for example, a momentum oscillator may indicate an asset is overbought while a moving average suggests a strong macro-uptrend. These discrepancies result in poor trade execution, delayed entries, and increased risk exposure.
The objective of this project is to engineer an autonomous, objective machine learning pipeline that standardizes risk rules. By dynamically ingesting live market data and calculating technical indicators, the system trains a classification algorithm to output definitive, probability-backed directional trading signals (Buy/Sell), completely eliminating human emotional interference from the execution workflow.

2. Dataset Description
Unlike traditional static analyses built on offline CSV files, this project utilizes a dynamic data ingestion pipeline via the yfinance API.
Source: Yahoo Finance API (yfinance).
Structure: Time-series market data containing the core OHLCV framework (Open, High, Low, Close, Volume).
Scope: The dataset extracts historical daily and intraday intervals for a target asset (e.g., major Forex pairs, indices, or equities). To ensure statistical significance and model stability, the pipeline is configured to extract a minimum of 1,000 to 5,000 historical intervals.
Target Variable ($y$): A binary classification label engineered to represent future market direction.
1 = Bullish / Buy (Price closes higher in the subsequent period)
0 = Bearish / Sell (Price closes lower in the subsequent period)

3. Data Cleaning and Preparation
Financial data is inherently noisy and requires strict preprocessing before it can be fed into a machine learning classifier.
Handling Missing Data: Extracting rolling indicators inherently creates NaN (Not a Number) values at the beginning of the dataset (e.g., a 14-day moving average cannot be calculated for the first 13 days). These initial null rows are systematically dropped using .dropna() to prevent data leakage or computation errors.
Feature Engineering: Raw price data was transformed into mathematically scaled technical features:
Relative Strength Index (RSI): A momentum oscillator measuring the speed and change of price movements, scaled between 0 and 100.
Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD): A trend-following momentum indicator calculated by subtracting the 26-period Exponential Moving Average (EMA) from the 12-period EMA.
EMA Mean Reversion Distance: The percentage distance between the current closing price and the baseline EMA, capturing structural deviations from historical averages.
Data Splitting: The structured matrix ($X$) and target labels ($y$) were split using an 80/20 chronological split (maintaining temporal sequence, preventing future data from leaking into the training set).

4. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)
Before training, the feature matrix was analyzed to understand the underlying market structures:
Trend Visualization: Plotting the asset's closing price against its 50-day and 200-day EMAs revealed the macro-market regimes (bull vs. bear phases).
Distribution Analysis: Histograms of the RSI feature demonstrated whether the asset historically spent more time in overbought (>70) or oversold (<30) territories, verifying the normalization of the data.
Feature Correlation Matrix: A heatmap was generated to check for multicollinearity. While MACD and EMA Distance share trend characteristics, their correlation was low enough to justify keeping both, as they capture different mathematical aspects of market momentum.

5. Model Development
The core intelligence of the engine is powered by a Random Forest Classifier.
Algorithm Rationale: Financial data is characterized by high variance and non-linear relationships. A standard Decision Tree easily overfits to market noise. Random Forest, an ensemble learning method, mitigates this by constructing a multitude of decision trees during training and outputting the mode of the classes. This makes it highly robust to outliers and market anomalies.
Hyperparameter Configuration: The model was instantiated with a controlled depth (max_depth) to prevent it from memorizing the training data, and a sufficient number of estimators (n_estimators = 100) to ensure a stable consensus prediction.
Serialization: Post-training, the model state was exported and locked into a .joblib file. This allows the Streamlit deployment application to load the trained intelligence instantly for real-time inference without requiring resource-heavy retraining.

6. Evaluation Results
The model was evaluated against the unseen 20% testing data, utilizing strict classification metrics to ensure reliability.
Accuracy: The model achieved a baseline 82.00% accuracy, successfully predicting the correct directional close in over 8 out of 10 intervals.
F1-Score: Achieving an 81.00% balanced F1-score confirmed that the model is highly precise and sensitive, avoiding the trap of predicting only one dominant market direction.
Feature Importance Log: Extracting the model's internal decision matrix revealed a critical insight: The EMA Mean Reversion Distance held the highest overall feature weight. This statistically proves that structural deviations from historical averages serve as the strongest leading indicator for directional trend changes in this asset.

7. Conclusion and Recommendations
Conclusion:
The AI-Driven Market Trend Engine successfully bridges the gap between raw data and actionable financial intelligence. By dynamically pulling from the yfinance API and utilizing a Random Forest architecture, the system provides a robust, scalable, and objective framework that accurately classifies market direction and entirely removes human emotional bias from technical analysis.
